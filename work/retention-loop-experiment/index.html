How I turned a dead end into active search

Growth case study · Activation recovery

I redesigned a key Getmancar scenario, turning a funnel dead end into a user-retention mechanism. This solution can help protect at least 23% of new customers from dropping off at the final step before their first booking.

Context

Getmancar is car-sharing in a smartphone: find a car, unlock it, drive

The business ask was to increase conversion to the first trip. Before designing anything, I needed to understand who we were trying to influence and where exactly in the journey.

Who is our “retained” user?

Before talking about improving conversion to the first trip, I first needed to understand what it actually means to activate and retain in car-sharing. I ran 6 in-depth interviews with active Getmancar users and mapped 5 core use cases based on them. Each had its own usage frequency, problem, and retention moment.

By comparing the qualitative research artifact with the natural usage frequency we saw in quantitative data, we found an important pattern: on average, 50% of active users do not take another trip the next month, but across a year, churn is no more than 10%.

In other words, people do come back, just not often: a large share of users may rent once every 3 months. This matched the “taxi alternative under specific conditions” use case: trips that happen in bursts, when a taxi is inconvenient or too expensive.

This use case covered the broadest audience segment, where an active user can be defined as someone who rents a car once every 3 months, at least 4 times a year.

Where the funnel broke

In this case, the main focus is the part of the funnel where the user has successfully completed the setup moment

The user has already registered, added a driver’s license, and waited for verification — in other words, this is someone with a high level of intent who has already invested significant effort and has only one step left: choose a car. But exactly here, 47.7% of verified users still did not start their first booking.

It became clear that our task was not to convince people to try Getmancar, because they were already doing that. We needed to preserve the intent users had already built at this stage.

Problem framing

In roughly half of these drop-off cases, there simply was no car nearby that the user could take right now — which meant at least 23% potential impact.

At first glance, this looks like an operations problem, but for a new user it very quickly becomes a product problem. If the first serious contact with Getmancar is “just an empty map”, the person does not get a chance to understand how the service could help them next time. As a result, the user’s mental model does not change, and taxi remains the “safer” and “more reliable” alternative.

At that moment, the product did not just fail to find a car. It failed to give the user a clear answer to the question: “What should I do next?”

The first idea

Offer a push notification that fires as soon as a free car appears nearby

But the more I thought about the user’s real context, the weaker this idea felt. A push that arrives at a random moment can easily be irrelevant. If a car appears at 8 a.m. while the person is in a meeting, they simply dismiss it.

After the same thing happens a few times, the user starts ignoring these pushes, disables them, or deletes the app without ever understanding its value.

This solution addressed the symptom, but completely ignored the user’s context. It could temporarily increase reach, but over time it would damage trust.

Product hypothesis and insight

If we let users who do not have a car nearby start a search while keeping control over the process, then those who use it will return to the app more often, and the number of first and repeat-trip bookings will increase.

Getmancar already had Car Radar: a feature with search zone settings and filters for car parameters. But in in-depth interviews, all active users said:

“I knew it existed. But I never used it”

People perceived this tool as something too complex. At the moment when you simply want to find a car and go, having to choose a radius, configure filters, and calculate the search area created extra mental load. The feature was useful, but overloaded, so I needed to find a simpler solution.

The final flow

Quick Search is the same Car Radar idea, but without any setup

The flow is intentionally simple and answers only one user question: “What can I do if I do not see any cars nearby?”

01

When there are no cars nearby, the app no longer shows just an empty map. Instead, it shows a message: “There are no cars nearby right now. We’ll let you know as soon as one appears” — and one “Notify me” button.

02

We keep the search under the user’s control: if the user agrees, active search starts and runs for 15 minutes by default. The user can extend it for another 15 minutes, pause it, or stop it. This makes the system predictable and avoids overloading the user with extra decisions.

03

It brings the user back with enough context to decide. When a car appears and the user returns to the app, they see the route to the car, walking distance in minutes, model, fuel level, transmission, and a choice: skip it or open details and move toward booking.

The key difference from Car Radar: zero decisions upfront. One action, and the search is running.

Outside the app

Dynamic Island as an extension of the control idea

Its goal:

Expected impact

Growth in first-trip conversion among verified users

The app stops leading people into an empty state and gives them a reason to return.

↑First booking

More verified users who did not have cars nearby move to a concrete car offer and booking intent.

↑Push returns

Push becomes a direct reactivation channel for people who already had intent, but did not have a car available.

↑Car details opens

Suggested car opens show whether the offer has enough context to move users from passive waiting to evaluation.

!Guardrails

It is important to watch ignored offers, stop or pause rate, notification opt-out, and uninstall after using Quick Search.

Long term, this can shift the mental model: if a person solves their problem through Getmancar for the first time, the next time they are in a similar situation they are more likely to open Getmancar first, not a taxi app.

Validation and reflection

So before implementation, if the team had capacity for usability testing, I would check:

01

Comprehension: does the user understand that Getmancar starts searching for a car when the timer appears?

02

Control: is it clear that the search can be extended, paused, or stopped, and that it does not book a car automatically?

03

Trust in the offer: does the found-car notification feel like a useful interaction, not a random or intrusive push?

What I took away from this case:

This case shows that a funnel should be read not only quantitatively, as a sequence of screens and drop-off points, but also qualitatively, as shifts in users’ mental states. A 47.7% drop-off after verification did not simply point to a loss at the car-selection step. These were people who had already invested effort and formed intent, so the solution did not need to persuade them of Getmancar’s value again. It needed to preserve that intent at the moment when they could not act on it.

The second learning is that even seemingly obvious growth mechanics need to be checked against the real usage context. Push notifications looked like an obvious solution that should work. But without user control, the whole mechanic becomes irritating. That is why the idea transformed into Quick Search: a mechanic that helps users control the search process exactly when they need it.
